Nigeria’s Food and Beverage Sector Eyes Massive Growth as agrofood Nigeria 2026 Returns to Lagos
As West Africa’s largest economy braces for explosive population growth, agrofood Nigeria 2026 arrives in Lagos at a moment when the stakes – and the opportunities – have never been higher.
Lagos, Nigeria – A market projected to reach $233.53 billion in 2025. A population on track to nearly double within a single generation. And a food trade deficit that lays bare the distance between where Nigeria’s food industry stands today and where it needs to be tomorrow.
The Nigerian food market is growing at an annual rate of 10.76% from 2025 to 2030, a pace that outstrips most major economies and signals one of the largest agrifood investment opportunities anywhere on the African continent. Yet despite significant investments in domestic production, Nigeria’s food imports still totaled US$6.1 billion in 2023, dwarfing food exports of just US$2.2 billion, according to the World Trade Organization. The gap is enormous. So is the ambition to close it.
Against this backdrop, agrofood Nigeria returns to the Landmark Centre on Victoria Island in Lagos from March 24 to 26, 2026 – the 11th edition of a trade show that has quietly become the most important annual gathering for the food and beverage value chain in West Africa.
A Presidential Stamp of Approval
agrofood Nigeria is no newcomer. In 2024, the event earned a powerful endorsement when Chief Olusegun Obasanjo, former President of the Federal Republic of Nigeria, delivered the opening speech – a moment that reflected more than ceremonial recognition; it signaled the platform’s alignment with broader continental ambitions around industrialization and value-chain development.
Two years on, the brand has only strengthened. The event has established itself across key markets on the African continent, including Côte d’Ivoire and Ethiopia, and the 2026 edition is shaping up to be the most international yet.
The Netherlands Takes Centre Stage
This year’s edition features The Netherlands as Guest of Honor – a designation that reflects the deepening bilateral partnership between the two countries in the agrofood sector. Dutch expertise in horticulture, dairy technology, and sustainable food systems makes the Netherlands a natural partner for Nigeria’s modernization drive.
National pavilions and participations from Belgium’s Flanders Region, China, Germany, India, and South Africa have already been confirmed, turning the exhibition floor into a compressed global marketplace where technology transfer, sourcing, and deal-making happen in real time.
Why Nigeria, Why Now?
The numbers demand attention. Nigeria is the largest importer of food and packaging technologies in West Africa, with imports of food and packaging technology reaching €265 million in 2024, according to VDMA. Packaging technology imports alone accounted for €121 million in the same year.
Behind these figures lies a structural reality: a population of roughly 238 million in 2025, projected to swell to 411 million by 2050, is generating demand that the current food production and processing infrastructure simply cannot meet. Urbanization is accelerating. A swelling middle class is shifting consumption patterns toward packaged, branded, and convenience products. And every link in the value chain – from field to fork – is under pressure to scale.
For international technology providers and local producers alike, the trajectory is unmistakable: massive, sustained investment in processing capacity, cold chain infrastructure, packaging innovation, and industrial-scale ingredient sourcing is not optional. It is inevitable.
From Farm to Factory: The Technology Gap
Upstream, agricultural technology is finally attracting meaningful capital. Nigerian agritech startups raised over $100 million in venture funding in 2024–2025, according to Disrupt Africa, with innovations spanning drone-based crop monitoring, solar-powered irrigation, and precision farming. The government’s National Agricultural Technology and Innovation Policy is channeling additional public funds into mechanization and post-harvest loss reduction.
Yet the gap between farm output and industrial input remains vast. Post-harvest losses in Nigeria still exceed 30% for perishable goods, according to the Food and Agriculture Organization – a staggering figure that represents both a humanitarian challenge and a commercial opportunity for storage, logistics, and processing technology providers.
Hospitality and Food Service: The Demand Pull
Downstream, Nigeria’s food hospitality market is adding further momentum. Lagos alone has seen a wave of hotel developments, quick-service restaurant chains, and catering operations serving a young, urban population with rising disposable incomes. The International Hotel & Restaurant Association estimates that Nigeria’s food service market will grow at 8–9% annually through the end of the decade.
That expansion creates cascading demand for standardized ingredients, reliable cold chain logistics, and industrial-scale food preparation equipment – precisely the segments where global suppliers are competing for market share.
More Than a Trade Show
What distinguishes agrofood Nigeria from other industry gatherings is the depth of its institutional backing. The event is supported by more than 20 respected organizations, from international chambers of commerce and Nigerian government ministries to trade agencies such as the German Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and industry bodies like VDMA and NACCIMA.
This broad coalition reinforces the show’s credibility as West Africa’s leading business and innovation hub for the agrofood and packaging sectors. Year after year, it generates thousands of high-value business contacts, connecting exhibitors with professional buyers, distributors, and decision-makers across the entire food and beverage value chain.
A Platform Anchored in West Africa’s Regional Market
agrofood Nigeria has evolved beyond a national trade fair into a platform that commands participation across West Africa’s integrated food economy. Held in Lagos, the commercial nerve centre of the sub-region, the event draws manufacturers, processors, distributors, and technology providers not only from Nigeria but also from across ECOWAS markets, where demand for food processing, packaging innovation, and agricultural modernization is accelerating.
Free Entry – and a Clear Signal
In a move designed to maximize industry participation, agrofood Nigeria 2026 is free of charge for professional visitors. It is a deliberate signal: the organizers want the widest possible cross-section of Nigeria’s food industry professionals to access world-class solutions, from farm-level technology to retail-ready packaging.
For businesses looking to enter or expand in the Nigerian market, and for local operators seeking global expertise, the platform is open. The infrastructure is in place. The institutional support is stronger than ever.
The only remaining question is whether the industry will match the scale of its ambition to the scale of the opportunity.
agrofood Nigeria 2026 takes place March 24–26 at Landmark Centre, Victoria Island, Lagos. Registration and further details are available at www.agrofood-nigeria.com
